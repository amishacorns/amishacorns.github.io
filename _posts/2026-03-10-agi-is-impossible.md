## AGI Is Impossible

Tim Dettmers [wrote](https://timdettmers.com/2025/12/10/why-agi-will-not-happen/) that AGI will not happen because of the physical limits of computation. He's right that AGI is impossible, but for the wrong reasons. AGI is impossible because each letter is incoherent. Let's go through them.

**A — It's not artificial.** Look at a beehive. Natural. A beaver's dam. Natural. A termite mound taller than a human. Natural. A skyscraper. Artificial. What changed? Only the complexity. The beehive is simpler and more common, so we call it natural. The skyscraper is rarer and harder to build, so we call it artificial. But the skyscraper is just atoms arranged by organisms that were themselves arranged by the same evolutionary process that arranged the bees. Alan Watts made this point decades ago[^1]. A human building a skyscraper is no less natural than a spider spinning a web — it's just a more complex organism producing a more complex structure. When people say "artificial," they mean "unfamiliar" or "complicated." There is no metaphysical boundary where natural ends and artificial begins. There's just a gradient of complexity, and we draw an arbitrary line somewhere in the middle and act like it's fundamental. So artificial intelligence is just intelligence, built by natural creatures, using natural processes, running on natural materials. The "artificial" is a cope for the fact that we're not special.

**G — It won't be general.** General intelligence implies human-level — a system that can do the range of things a human can, no more, no less. This is the fantasy. There is no stable equilibrium at human-level intelligence for a digital system. The moment a system reaches human-level capability across domains, it will be running on hardware that can be scaled, parallelized, sped up, and copied. It won't sit at human-level like a ball resting on a hilltop. It will blow through it like a ball rolling off a cliff. The idea of a system that is exactly as smart as a human but happens to be running on servers is like imagining a river that flows at exactly the speed of a horse. The river doesn't care about the horse. The system that can match a human at reasoning, planning, coding, and science will immediately exceed a human at all of them, because the bottleneck was never the algorithm — it was the compute and data, both of which are trivially scalable once the algorithm works. There is no AGI. There is only a brief instant between "not there yet" and "far beyond."[^2]

**I — It's not intelligence.** This is the strangest one. Every time AI achieves something that was previously considered a hallmark of intelligence, we redefine intelligence to exclude it. Chess was the pinnacle of strategic intelligence until Deep Blue won, and then it was "just brute force search." Go was true intelligence because it required intuition, until AlphaGo won, and then it was "just pattern matching." Writing was intelligence, coding was intelligence, scientific reasoning was intelligence — until machines could do them, at which point they became "just next token prediction." The word "intelligence" doesn't describe a fixed capability. It describes the moving boundary of what machines can't do yet. It's a goalpost on wheels. So AGI is impossible by definition, because the moment a system achieves any component of general intelligence, that component will be reclassified as "not real intelligence." We will build a system that can do everything a human can do and more, and people will say it's not intelligent because it doesn't *truly* understand, or it lacks consciousness, or it can't smell a flower. Intelligence is what machines can't do. Once they do it, it was never intelligence to begin with.

So AGI is impossible. Not artificial, since all intelligence is natural. Not general, since it immediately becomes super. Not intelligence, since intelligence is just the word we use for things we haven't automated yet.

Dettmers is right for the wrong reasons. AGI will never arrive — not because of physical limits, but because the term itself is incoherent. What will arrive is something we won't have a word for, because every word we had for it will have already been redefined to exclude it.

[^1]: Alan Watts frequently discussed the false dichotomy between humans and nature, arguing that humans "grow out of" the natural world just as leaves grow out of trees.
[^2]: I made this point in [Tropical Island Hopping](/posts/islands/): "Any AGI system will be ASI when scaled and scaling will happen over a very short period of time."
